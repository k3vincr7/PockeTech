Field Guide
🧪 PRESSURE

Pressure Measurement

Gauge, absolute, differential — know your reference before you touch anything.

Pressure Types
Three references, three different readings
PSIG
Gauge — referenced to atmosphere. Most common in process plants.
PSIA
Absolute — referenced to perfect vacuum. Used in vacuum systems and vapor pressure.
DP / PSID
Differential — measures the difference between two pressures. Used for flow and level.
⚠️ At sea level: PSIA = PSIG + 14.7. A transmitter configured for gauge reads 0 at atmospheric pressure — not 14.7.
How It Works
Signal path from process to DCS
🧪 Process Connection
Process pressure acts on the transmitter sensing diaphragm through an impulse line or direct mount.
🔌 Sensing Cell
The diaphragm deflects proportionally to pressure. A capacitive or piezoresistive element converts deflection to an electrical signal.
📈 Output
Electronics scale the signal to 4–20 mA. 4 mA = LRV (low range), 20 mA = URV (high range).
Impulse Lines
What connects the transmitter to the process
🔧 Block Valve
Isolates the transmitter from the process. Must be fully open during normal operation.
🔧 Block-and-Bleed Manifold
Allows isolation and venting of the transmitter without disturbing the process. Use it to zero-check in place.
⚠️ Plugged Lines
Solids, wax, or freezing can plug the impulse line — the reading sticks at the last value. Slow response is the first sign.
Quick Troubleshooting
🔴 Reading stuck — not responding to process changes
→ Plugged impulse line or closed block valve. Check manifold and tap condition.
🔴 Reading consistently low
→ Leak in impulse line or process connection. Check for visible weeping at fittings.
🟠 Reading has a constant offset (always high or low by a fixed amount)
→ Zero calibration error. Isolate, vent, and re-zero at the transmitter with no process pressure applied.
🔴 Reading noisy or unstable
→ Turbulent process, vibration, or loose process connection. Check mounting and configure damping.
🔴 Reading shows full-scale or goes to FAULT
→ Overpressure event may have damaged the sensing cell. Check process history and compare against transmitter URL.
Field Tips
💡 Always verify your reference point first — atmospheric, vacuum, or another pressure point. That one question tells you which transmitter type you need.
💡 To zero-check in the field: close the block valve, open the bleed, and verify the transmitter reads 0 (for gauge). If it doesn't, note the offset before adjusting.
💡 Never exceed the transmitter's URL (Upper Range Limit) — even briefly. Overpressure shifts the zero permanently and can rupture the diaphragm.
