Level Tank Type Descriptions

Quick field reference for choosing Open Tank, Closed Tank Dry Leg, or Closed Tank Wet Leg in PockeTech.

Open Tank

Open Tank

An open tank is vented to atmosphere. The low side of the DP transmitter is normally open to atmosphere, so the transmitter mainly sees the liquid head pushing on the high side.

  • Use when the tank is not pressurized or is vented.
  • LP side reference is usually atmospheric pressure.
  • LRV is commonly 0 inH₂O if the transmitter is mounted level with the lower tap and no offset is used.
  • URV is based on tap-to-tap span × liquid specific gravity.
Basic idea: dP = liquid height × process SG
Example:
Tank span = 100 in
Process SG = 1.0
LRV = 0 inH₂O
URV = 100 inH₂O
At 50% level, dP = 50 inH₂O and signal = 12 mA.
Closed Tank — Dry Leg

Closed Tank — Dry Leg

A closed dry leg setup is used on a pressurized vessel when the vapor/gas pressure is applied to the low side, but the low-side impulse line is dry.

  • Use when the vessel is pressurized and the vapor space connects to the LP side.
  • The pressure above the liquid is applied to both sides, so it cancels out.
  • The transmitter still mainly reads the liquid level head.
  • Best when vapors do not condense in the low-side impulse line.
Basic idea: HP = vessel pressure + liquid head; LP = vessel pressure; dP = liquid head
Example:
Closed vessel pressure = 20 psi
Liquid head at 50% = 50 inH₂O
HP sees vessel pressure + 50 inH₂O
LP sees vessel pressure only
The vessel pressure cancels out, so the transmitter reads about 50 inH₂O.
Closed Tank — Wet Leg

Closed Tank — Wet Leg

A closed wet leg setup is used when the low-side impulse line is intentionally filled with a stable reference liquid. This is common when vapors can condense or when the LP leg must stay full.

  • Use when the LP side has a filled reference leg.
  • The wet leg pushes down on the LP side all the time.
  • This often creates a negative LRV.
  • URV is the process liquid head minus the wet leg head, adjusted for any transmitter elevation offset.
Basic idea: dP = process liquid head − wet leg head + TX offset head
Example:
Tap span = 72 in
Process SG = 0.9
Wet leg height = 72 in
Wet leg SG = 1.0
LRV = 0 − 72 = -72 inH₂O
URV = (72 × 0.9) − 72 = -7.2 inH₂O
At 50% level, dP is halfway between -72 and -7.2 = -39.6 inH₂O.

TX Elevation Offset

Use offset when the transmitter is not mounted exactly at the lower tap elevation.

  • Positive offset: transmitter is below the lower tap, adding head.
  • Negative offset: transmitter is above the lower tap, subtracting head.
Positive Offset — TX Below Lower Tap
If the transmitter is mounted below the lower tap, extra liquid head is sitting above the transmitter. Enter this as a positive offset.
Negative Offset — TX Above Lower Tap
If the transmitter is mounted above the lower tap, it removes head from the measurement. Enter this as a negative offset.
Wet Leg Visual — LP Side Has Constant Head
Wet leg head is always pushing on the LP side. That is why wet-leg ranges often start negative.
Offset example:
Open tank span = 100 in, SG = 1.0
TX mounted 10 in below lower tap → offset = +10
LRV = +10 inH₂O
URV = +110 inH₂O

TX mounted 10 in above lower tap → offset = -10
LRV = -10 inH₂O
URV = +90 inH₂O
