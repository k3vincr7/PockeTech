🧥 TANK TYPE GUIDE

Tank Type Reference

Open, Closed Dry Leg, Closed Wet Leg — when to use each and what the math looks like.

Open Tank
Open Tank
Tank is vented to atmosphere. LP side is open to air — only liquid head affects the reading.
  • Use when the tank is not pressurized or is vented to atmosphere
  • LP side reference is atmospheric pressure
  • LRV = 0 inH₂O if the transmitter is at the same elevation as the lower tap (no offset)
  • URV = tap-to-tap span × process specific gravity
dP = liquid height × process SG
Example:
Tap span = 100 in  |  Process SG = 1.0
LRV = 0 inH₂O    URV = 100 inH₂O
At 50% level → dP = 50 inH₂O → 12 mA
Closed Tank — Dry Leg
Closed Tank — Dry Leg
Pressurized vessel with the vapor space connected to the LP side through a dry (gas-filled) impulse line.
  • Use when the vessel is pressurized and vapor won’t condense in the LP leg
  • Tank pressure appears on both HP and LP sides — it cancels out
  • The transmitter reads only the liquid head, same as open tank math
  • If vapors condense in the LP leg, readings will shift — switch to wet leg
HP = vessel pressure + liquid head    LP = vessel pressure
dP = liquid head  (vessel pressure cancels)
Example:
Vessel pressure = 20 psi  |  Liquid head at 50% = 50 inH₂O
HP = 20 psi + 50 inH₂O    LP = 20 psi
dP = 50 inH₂O  → vessel pressure cancels completely
Closed Tank — Wet Leg
Closed Tank — Wet Leg
LP impulse line is intentionally filled with a stable reference liquid. Used when process vapor may condense.
  • Use when vapors can condense in the LP leg or the leg must stay consistently full
  • The wet leg exerts a constant downward head on the LP side
  • This constant head usually makes the LRV negative
  • URV = process liquid head minus wet leg head (± TX elevation offset)
dP = process liquid head − wet leg head ± TX offset head
Example:
Tap span = 72 in  |  Process SG = 0.9  |  Wet leg height = 72 in  |  Wet leg SG = 1.0
LRV = 0 − 72 = −72 inH₂O
URV = (72 × 0.9) − 72 = 64.8 − 72 = −7.2 inH₂O
At 50% → dP = −39.6 inH₂O
Transmitter Elevation Offset
When the TX isn’t at the lower tap elevation
Any difference in height between the transmitter and the lower tap adds or subtracts a constant head from every reading.
⬇️ TX Below Lower Tap — Positive Offset
Extra liquid column sits above the transmitter inlet. Add this head to LRV and URV. Enter as a positive value.
⬆️ TX Above Lower Tap — Negative Offset
The transmitter is elevated above the tap — it sees less head. Subtract this from LRV and URV. Enter as a negative value.
Positive Offset — TX Below Lower Tap
Extra liquid head sits above the TX inlet. Add this height as a positive offset to both LRV and URV.
Negative Offset — TX Above Lower Tap
TX is above the lower tap — less head reaches it. Subtract this height as a negative offset from both LRV and URV.
Wet Leg — LP Side Has Constant Head
Wet leg head pushes down on LP side constantly — that’s why wet leg ranges typically start negative.
Offset examples:
Open tank span = 100 in  |  SG = 1.0

TX 10 in below lower tap → offset = +10
LRV = +10 inH₂O    URV = +110 inH₂O

TX 10 in above lower tap → offset = −10
LRV = −10 inH₂O    URV = +90 inH₂O
Quick Reference
💡 Open tank = simplest math. LRV is typically 0, URV = span × SG.
💡 Dry leg = same math as open tank, but only valid if nothing condenses in the LP leg. If it does, you get a false reading.
💡 Wet leg = LRV is almost always negative. The wet leg head is always working against the process level on the LP side.
💡 Offset only applies if the TX is not at the lower tap elevation. Most direct-mount transmitters have no offset.
