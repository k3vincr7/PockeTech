📈 4–20 mA

4–20 mA Basics

What it is, how it works in the field, and the math behind it.

The Fundamentals
What is 4–20 mA?

A 4–20 mA signal is a current loop used to represent a measurement. Current — not voltage — so wire resistance doesn't affect the reading.

4 mA  = 0%  — Low range (LRV). Signal is alive at 4, not 0.
12 mA = 50% — Always. Memorize this one.
20 mA = 100% — High range (URV)
Below 3.6 mA or above 21 mA = Fault / NAMUR alarm
How the Loop Works
Signal Path
🔋 Power Supply (24 VDC)
Drives the loop. Usually sourced from the DCS AI card or a dedicated loop supply in the panel.
🌟 Transmitter (TX)
Senses the process (pressure, level, temp, flow) and controls how much current flows — like a variable resistor.
🧠 PLC / DCS (AI Card)
Reads current through a precision resistor (250 Ω typical), converts it to voltage, then scales to engineering units.
🔄 Return Path
Current must complete the loop back to the supply. An open anywhere = below 4 mA = fault alarm at DCS.
Signal Math
Quick Conversions
mA → %    (mA − 4) ÷ 16 × 100
% → mA    4 + (% ÷ 100 × 16)
mA → EU   LRV + (mA − 4) ÷ 16 × span
Key Milestones
  • 4 mA = 0%
  • 8 mA = 25%
  • 12 mA = 50%
  • 16 mA = 75%
  • 20 mA = 100%
Real Field Example
Range: 0–100 PSI
  • TX sees 0 psi → sends 4 mA → DCS reads 0 psi
  • TX sees 50 psi → sends 12 mA → DCS reads 50 psi
  • TX sees 100 psi → sends 20 mA → DCS reads 100 psi
EU = LRV + (mA − 4) ÷ 16 × span
Example: 12 mA = 0 + (12−4) ÷ 16 × 100 = 50 psi
Field Tips
💡 12 mA is always 50% — regardless of range. Pressure, flow, level, temperature. Use it to sanity-check any reading instantly.
💡 Reading below 3.6 mA? Check for an open wire or blown fuse before touching the transmitter.
💡 Current is the same everywhere in the loop — measure it in series at any point and you'll get the same mA reading.
