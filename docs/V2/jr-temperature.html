🌡️ TEMPERATURE

Temperature (RTD & TC)

Two sensor types, two different principles — know which one you're working with.

Sensor Types
RTD vs Thermocouple
🔌 RTD
Resistance increases with temperature. Very accurate and stable. Common: PT100 (100 Ω at 0°C), PT1000.
⚡ Thermocouple (TC)
Two dissimilar metals generate millivolts. Wider range, faster response. Common types: J, K, T, E.
⚠️ These are not interchangeable. The transmitter or DCS input card must be configured for the correct sensor type — a wrong config gives a wrong reading with no alarm.
How RTDs Work
Wiring configurations
🔌 2-Wire RTD
Simplest wiring. Lead wire resistance adds directly to the reading — creates a positive offset error. Avoid on long cable runs.
🔌 3-Wire RTD
Third wire lets the transmitter measure and cancel lead resistance. Standard for most field installations.
🔌 4-Wire RTD
Fully eliminates lead resistance error. Used in lab or high-precision applications.
How Thermocouples Work
mV signal and cold junction compensation
⚡ Cold Junction Compensation (CJC)
A TC measures the difference between the hot junction (process) and the cold junction (terminal block). The transmitter measures terminal block temperature and compensates. If CJC fails, the reading drifts with ambient temperature.
🛡️ TC Extension Wire
Must match the TC type all the way from the sensor to the transmitter. Using regular copper wire for TC extension introduces a new unwanted junction and corrupts the measurement.
Quick Troubleshooting
🔴 Reading pegged at max / shows OPEN
→ Open circuit in sensor or lead wiring. Check continuity at the head. PT100 should read ~109 Ω at room temp (~25°C).
🔴 Reading pegged at min / shows SHORT
→ Short circuit in sensor leads. Check for moisture in the connection head or damaged insulation.
🟠 Reading off by a fixed amount — always high or low
→ 2-wire RTD lead resistance error, or wrong sensor type configured at the transmitter.
🟠 TC reading drifts with ambient temperature
→ Cold junction compensation failure, or copper wire used instead of proper TC extension wire.
🔴 Correct at one temp, wrong everywhere else
→ Wrong TC type selected (K configured, J installed). Always check the sensor identification tag.
Field Tips
💡 TC polarity matters. Reversed leads make the reading move opposite of actual temperature. In the US, red is usually negative on TC extension wire.
💡 Confirm sensor type before troubleshooting anything else. A K-type and J-type look identical physically — the only difference is the alloy and the tag.
💡 Never run TC extension wire in the same conduit as power wiring. Induced voltage causes erratic readings that are very hard to trace back to the source.
